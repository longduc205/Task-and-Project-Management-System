§ 3 · Timeline & Milestones

PERT three-point estimation. The formula is TE equals O plus 4M plus P, all divided by 6. Standard deviation is P minus O divided by 6. We applied this to all six network activities.

The critical path activities — A, B, E, G, H — are highlighted. Activity F is non-critical and carries eight days of float. The TE values are slightly higher than the most likely values for most activities, which is the point of PERT: optimistic estimates are rarely achieved, so the weighted average is more realistic.

The worked example at the bottom is Activity B — UI/UX Design. O equals 5, M equals 10, P equals 18. The weighted expected duration is 10.5 days. The standard deviation is 2.17 days. This means there's roughly a 68% probability that the activity will fall between 8.3 and 12.7 days. And indeed, as our lessons learned show, the actual duration was 14 days — above the PERT estimate. That's a key insight for next time.

§ 4 · Resource Management

The make-or-buy analysis. We applied the Chapter 9 framework to every significant technology decision. The result is a clear pattern: we buy for framework-level decisions — React, Vite, TypeScript, react-router-dom, Vitest — and we build for the differentiating layers — the custom UI components and the data persistence layer.

The rationale for each buy decision is documented in the table. React gives us a mature ecosystem, strong typing, and fast development. Vitest is industry-standard for TypeScript projects. Routing via react-router-dom is the de facto standard for React SPAs.

We build the UI components and data layer for two reasons: full control over the bundle size and visual design, and — honestly — this is a student project, and building these things is the learning objective.

One important note: we initially planned to use Supabase as a cloud backend, but CR-001 changed this decision at Week 6. We descoped it to local state, which eliminated setup complexity and improved demo reliability.

§ 6 · Conflict Resolution · Ch. 12

6-step process — agreed before the first conflict arose

1
Identify the root causes
PM calls a private session. Each party states their position in one sentence. What is the underlying source of the disagreement — not the symptom?
2
Gather facts from everyone
Collect objective data — task logs, commit history, WBS definitions. No hearsay. The facts, not opinions, drive the discussion.
3
Facilitate open group discussion
All parties present their perspective. Moderated by PM. Time-boxed to 15 minutes per issue to prevent rabbit-holes.
4
Focus on issues, not personalities
PM enforces ground rules. Redirect from "you said…" to "the decision was…". Avoid blame; stay on-topic. Blame derails resolution.
5
Seek collaborative win-win resolutions
Find solutions that satisfy all parties — trade-offs, task swaps, timeline adjustments. Document the trade-off rationale for future reference.
6
Document agreements immediately
Added to meeting minutes within 24 hours. All parties confirm via Teams. The record prevents the same argument recurring next month.

Agreement reached before Week 1: "If we can't resolve a disagreement through steps 1–6 within 48 hours, the PM makes the final decision with documented rationale. All parties commit to supporting the decision."

§ 6 · Communication & Collaboration

The six-step conflict resolution process from Chapter 12. We agreed to this process in Week 1, before any conflict arose. That's intentional — the worst time to negotiate a process is in the heat of a disagreement.

The process is structured: identify root causes first — not symptoms — then gather facts objectively, facilitate open discussion, enforce issue-focused ground rules, seek win-win resolutions, and document everything immediately. The documentation step is often skipped in student projects but it's critical — it prevents the same argument from recurring three weeks later.

We also agreed on an escalation path: if the six steps don't resolve a disagreement within 48 hours, the PM makes the final decision with documented rationale. Everyone commits to supporting that decision. This prevents deadlocks.
